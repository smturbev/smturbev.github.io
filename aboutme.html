About Me

Current Position

University of Washington Research Assistant

I am a third year graduate student in Atmospheric Sciences at the University of Washington working with Tom Ackerman, Chris Bretherton, Peter Blossey and Jacqueline Nugent. Under the NSF PIRE grant, my work is closely tied to others working in the field of TTL cirrus. We have weekly reading group and/or presentations about related work (For more info contact me).

I am also involved in the python club in atmos at UW. For tutorials and resources check out this page. We cover topics such as conda and virtual environments, personal webpages creation, jupyterlab and notebook, and more. This is an on-going effort with Aeodhan Sweeney, Claire Zarakas, and Jacqueline Nugent with help from everyone in the atmos-python club.

Background

Colby College

My undergraduate degree is in Mathematical Sciences from Colby College in Maine. There, my scientific inquiry began. I worked with Scott Taylor in the Math department studying knot theory which turned into research on Dehn Twists. While I didn't produce any impact in the field, it sparked my interest in research sciences. I turned away from theoretical mathematics and toward a more applied science that spoke to my roots and appreciation for nature. Thus, I applied for graduate school in Atmospheric Sciences.

My roots

Growing up in Pukalani, Hawaii, my love and respect for the environment and science began.

As a BIPOC scientist in one of the whitest STEM fields, I strive to create an more equitable field #BlackLivesMatter. I am interested in both the radiation of the atmosphere (specifically dealing with tropical clouds) and the radiation of baking (with my sourdough starter named DoughVID19). My research focuses on the radiative properties of TTL cirrus and the lifecycle of tropical systems in high resolution models. To stay sane during lockdown, I baked lots and lots of sourdough bread from rosemary crackers to naturally leavened boules which I have continued as things open up again.
